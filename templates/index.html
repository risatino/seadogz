OVERVIEW: An Interactive Comparison Data Report

The goal of this project was to compare basketball outcomes data (games stats & injury data) to captured athlete monitoring data and evaluate for trends. Data was compiled from the Catapult Sport API, team game statistics, and injury data from one NCAA Division I team. Data exploration was completed in Python Pandas with data visualization in Tableau and machine learning in Sci-Kit Learn. The graphs and data below show our exploration and analysis.


Acute to chronic load ratios provide a snapshot of an athlete’s ‘fitness’ and ‘fatigue’. There are different ways to calculate the ratio; we used the rolling average model. A handful of studies state that hovering around a ratio of 1.0 is a sweet spot for injury prevention, whereas, over 1.5 increases the likelihood of injury. The above graph compares two different versions of how this ratio. The gray line is the Science of Sport recommended measure of (7) days for the acute calculation and (28) days for chronic calculation. The green line shows the Catapult Sport calculated ration of (4) days to (28) days.

The Science of Sport model has less variance throughout the season than Catapult Sport calculations. For future analysis, a team or individual specific acute to chronic ratio threshold will help team staff plan optimal workouts and recovery measures. Current limitations is spotty wearable data.

Using acute:chronic ratio, and, more broadly, aggregate team load data exhibits limitations in predicting the score of a game. For example, when the team load averaged between 5,000-6,000, the points scored ranged from 61 to 86 points. Similarly, on the high end, when the team load averaged over 8,000 for a game, the range in points scored was 60 to 96.

Predicting game outcome presents comparable limitations. Losses over the 2016-17 and 2017-18 seasons exhibited an average aggregate team load ranging from 4,923 to 10,605, while wins over the same period ranged from 5,263 to 10,072.

There are a number of potential reasons driving the limitation of using aggregate average team load data to predict game score or outcome. One such theory stems from the fact that not all teams present the same level of matchup difficulty. Strength of schedule would logically play a significant role in determining the level of effort needed to score points or obtain victory. Further research is needed to better determine how matchup affects game load.
